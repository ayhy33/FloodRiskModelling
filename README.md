# Abstract
This research investigates the temporal relationship between rainfall and river water levels using high-resolution time series data collected across five catchments in Scotland from 2023 to 2024. Data were collected at 15-minute intervals by Scottish Environment Protection Agency (SEPA) monitoring stations and retrieved via their public API, providing fine-grained insights into catchment hydrology. Pearson correlation and time-lagged cross-correlation were employed to investigate how rainfall influences water level over time for each catchment selected for this research. The observed lag, which varied across catchments, reflects the combined influence of physical characteristics including land cover, soil type, geology and terrain. These factors affect how rainfall is absorbed, stored and routed through the catchment before contributing to river flow. The lag values guided the structure of predictive models using Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural networks. Both univariate (rainfall-only) and multivariate (rainfall and water level) models were developed. Model performance was assessed using the coefficient of determination (𝑅²), Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE). Multivariate models consistently outperformed univariate ones in this research, due to the added value of incorporating both rainfall and prior water levels. Additionally, zonal statistics were applied to extract spatial characteristics within each catchment, including metrics such as area size, dominant land cover type (e.g., arable, suburban, heather) and the variety of land cover classes. This analysis helps to understand how different land types and their spatial arrangements influence hydrological responses, such as how quickly or slowly rainfall translates into changes in river water levels. For example, areas dominated by permeable soils or vegetation (like arable land) may exhibit delayed runoff due to greater infiltration, whereas urban or suburban zones tend to generate quicker responses due to impermeable surfaces. This research demonstrates the benefit of integrating spatial and temporal analyses for improving flood forecasting, offering a reproducible framework applicable to both small and large catchment areas.
